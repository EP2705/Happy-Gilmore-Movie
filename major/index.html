Happy Gilmore is a 1996 American sports comedy film. Dennis Dugan directed the film, which was produced by Robert Simonds and stars Adam Sandler, Christopher McDonald, Julie Bowen, and Carl Weathers. It portrays the story of an unsuccessful ice hockey player who discovers a new talent for golf and enters a golf tournament to save his grandmother's home from eviction. Sandler and his writing partner Tim Herlihy wrote the screenplay, their second movie collaboration after the 1995 film Billy Madison.

Fun Facts
  • Happy Gilmore is based on Adam Sandlers childhood friend Kyle. Kyle was a hockey player who became really passionate about the game. He also frequently played golf with Sandler and his father. 
  • Adam Sandler performed several of his own stunts, including his signature powerful golf swing.
  • Christopher McDonald, who played Shooter McGavin, declined the part twice, claiming he was bored of playing villains. He changed his mind after meeting with Sandler.
  • It was a commercial success, generating over $41 million domestically and developing a cult following over the years.
  • Adam Sandler is currently developing a sequel to Happy Gilmore 27 years later
Impact

Infamous Swing
Happy Gilmore has left a significant impact on golf, one way being his infamous unorthodox swing. He performs this swing in this order:
  1. Instead of the traditional feet planted golf stance, he uses a running start
  2. He then takes a few steps towards the ball
  3. Finally unleashes his powerful drive

While the “Happy Gilmore swing” is not recommended for serious golfers, it did bring attention to the sport and introduced an element of fun and irreverence.

Increased Interest in Golf

The film’s popularity led to more people becoming curious about golf. Even those who had never considered playing before were intrigued by the idea of a hockey player turned golfer with an unconventional swing. Golf courses saw an uptick in interest, and some beginners even attempted their own versions of the Happy Gilmore swing.

Cultural Impact

“Happy Gilmore” injected humor and entertainment into a sport that can sometimes be seen as stuffy or exclusive.
The film’s portrayal of golf as both challenging and amusing resonated with audiences, making it more accessible and relatable.
